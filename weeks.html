Week 01 – Introduction to HCI

Week 01 introduced Human–Computer Interaction, explaining why usability and user-centered design are critical. HCI combines knowledge from computer science, psychology, and design to create usable systems (Dix et al., Chapter 1).

Key Points

  • Definition and scope of HCI
  • Importance of usability and user satisfaction
  • User-centered design approach
  • Multidisciplinary nature: psychology, design, computer science

Reflection

Understanding HCI basics helps frame future lab work and emphasizes designing for user needs.

Week 02 – Humans and Computers

Focus was on human capabilities and limitations affecting system design, including perception, memory, attention, and motor skills (Dix et al., Chapter 2).

Key Points

  • Human perception and sensory processing
  • Memory: short-term, long-term, working memory
  • Attention types: selective, divided, sustained
  • Motor skills and response time considerations

Reflection

Design must reduce cognitive load, prevent errors, and align with natural human behavior.

Week 03 – Interaction and Interface Design

Week 03 covered interaction styles and interface types. Interaction styles define how users communicate with systems (Dix et al., Chapter 4).

Interaction Styles

  • Command-line interfaces
  • Menu-based systems
  • Form-based interfaces
  • Direct manipulation interfaces

Reflection

Choosing the right interaction style reduces errors and improves satisfaction, e.g., direct manipulation for novices.

Week 04 – Errors and Usability Issues

Focus was on user errors and design strategies to prevent or recover from them (Dix et al., Chapter 8).

Types of Errors

  • Slips – execution errors
  • Mistakes – planning errors
  • Feedback mechanisms for error correction
  • Designing for recoverability and learnability

Reflection

Analyzing errors helps identify design flaws and improves usability through clear instructions and immediate feedback.
